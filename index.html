Personal Assessment

AI/ML Leadership and Change Management Reflection

A personal reflection on my leadership strengths, development areas, and readiness to support AI-driven organizational change.

01

Assessment Overview

After taking both the AI/ML leadership Change Management assessments, I understand my leadership strengths and growth areas much better. These tests proved that successfully using AI requires both technical skills and string leadership.

02

AI/ML Leadership Assessment

In the AI/ML assessment, my top strengths were my technical knowledge, problem solving skills, and love for learning. I enjoy exploring new technology and using it to solve business problems. However, I want to get better at leading big AI projects across different teams, measuring the business value of AI, and mentoring team members as they learn to use AI.

03

Change Management Assessment

In the change management assessment, my top strengths were adaptability, communication, and collaboration. Working on cloud and data engineering projects has taught me how important it is to adapt to changes while keeping everyone informed. On the other hand, I need to work on handling people who resist change, building better long-term plans, and influencing stakeholders during major company shifts.

04

Overall Reflection

Overall, these tests were great for self-reflection because they made me think about my leadership skills, not just my technical abilities. However, because I graded myself, the results might be a bit biased. Adding feedback from my manager or peers would make the results much more accurate. Finally, based on my reading. I believe both tests would be better if they used real world workplace scenarios instead of simple rating scales. I would also add questions about AI ethics, responsible AI rules, and how to build trust during big company changes

AI Evolution

How Artificial Intelligence Evolved

AI progressed from rule-based systems to machine learning, deep learning, generative AI, and intelligent business applications.

1950s–1980s

Rule-Based Artificial Intelligence

Early AI systems followed predefined rules and decision trees. They could solve structured problems but had limited ability to learn from new information.

1990s–2000s

Machine Learning

AI began learning patterns from historical data. Organizations used predictive models for classification, forecasting, fraud detection, and process optimization.

2010s

Deep Learning and Big Data

Increased computing power and larger datasets made advanced image recognition, natural language processing, and complex pattern detection possible.

2020s

Generative AI and Intelligent Assistants

Generative AI enabled systems to create text, images, code, summaries, and recommendations while supporting conversational experiences and knowledge retrieval.

Today

AI Integrated with Enterprise Data

Modern organizations combine AI with governed data platforms, analytics, automation, and human expertise to accelerate decisions and improve business outcomes.
